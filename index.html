Can therapeutic play decrease pain rating scores in pediatric patients experiencing cancer/chemo related pain when compared to pharmacological analgesics?

Studies have found that childhood cancer has a very high financial burden and leads “some patients and their families to make treatment decisions based on the associated cost” (Chae, 2020). A major component in the treatment of cancer is pain management. As such, a group of researchers in Spain set it upon themselves to determine what impact the use of electronic video games (EVGs) could have on pain relief in children with cancer. The researchers looked at 20 kids aged 4-17 who were hospitalized and were receiving treatment for acute pain related to mucositis, an inflammatory condition of the mouth and/or stomach which is common amongst cancer patients. The study took a quasi-experimental pre/posttest approach, meaning that they looked at metrics for the same kids prior to and after having played with games. This means that the study was evidence level II (out of V) and was of good quality (level B), but not high quality (for more information on evidence levels and quality follow this website).
A major barrier for pediatric cancer treatment is cost, which "may be driven by unexpected procedures during cancer treatment, including treatment of infections or pain" (Warner, 2014).

What were the results of the study and what do they mean for practice?

EVGs were played for a mean of 2.3 hours per day and lead to statistically significant changes. After playing, there was lower daily morphine use, lower demand for pain relief, and lower pain scores across the board. In summary, the researchers found that the use of EVGs has significant pain relief effect. The study was small, but it has inspired more researchers to investigate the effect of both electronic and non-electronic play therapy on pediatric cancer pain. If future evidence surges that suggests that this is an effective way of treating pain it could easily be implemented by the healthcare team and would reduce the cost-burden associated with pediatric cancer!
